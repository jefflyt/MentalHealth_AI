Warning Signs and When to Seek Help

Recognizing Warning Signs:
Knowing when you or someone you care about needs professional mental health support is crucial. Early intervention can prevent crisis situations and lead to better outcomes. This guide helps you identify warning signs and understand when to seek help.

Mental Health Warning Signs in Yourself:

**Emotional Changes:**
- Persistent sadness, hopelessness, or emptiness
- Excessive worry, fear, or anxiety
- Extreme mood swings
- Intense irritability or anger
- Feeling numb or emotionally detached
- Overwhelming guilt or worthlessness
- Loss of interest in things you used to enjoy

**Behavioral Changes:**
- Social withdrawal and isolation
- Significant changes in sleep (too much or too little)
- Appetite changes (eating too much or too little)
- Neglecting personal hygiene
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Procrastination or avoiding responsibilities
- Engaging in risky behaviors
- Increased use of alcohol or substances

**Physical Symptoms:**
- Unexplained aches and pains
- Frequent headaches
- Digestive problems
- Fatigue and low energy
- Restlessness or inability to sit still
- Physical tension or muscle pain

**Thinking Patterns:**
- Racing or intrusive thoughts
- Difficulty focusing or remembering
- Persistent negative thoughts
- Thoughts of death or dying
- Self-harm thoughts
- Feeling like a burden to others
- Believing things would be better without you

**Functioning Problems:**
- Difficulty performing at work or school
- Trouble maintaining relationships
- Can't complete daily tasks
- Missing work, school, or important commitments
- Feeling unable to cope with normal stress

When to Seek Professional Help:

**Seek Help Soon (Within Days/Weeks) If:**
- Symptoms persist for 2+ weeks
- Symptoms interfere with daily life
- You're using unhealthy coping mechanisms
- You're having frequent panic attacks
- Sleep problems are ongoing
- You're isolating yourself more than usual
- You notice significant mood or behavior changes
- Self-care is becoming difficult

**Seek Help Immediately (Within 24 Hours) If:**
- Thoughts of harming yourself or others
- Thoughts of suicide or making plans
- Hearing voices or seeing things others don't
- Engaging in self-harm behaviors
- Severe panic or anxiety attacks
- Complete inability to function
- Psychotic symptoms (losing touch with reality)
- Thoughts that others are trying to harm you
- Severe eating disorder behaviors

**Call Emergency Services (995) If:**
- Active suicide attempt or plan in progress
- Immediate risk of harm to self or others
- Experiencing a mental health emergency
- Overdose or medical emergency

Warning Signs in Others:

**Behavioral Red Flags:**
- Talking about wanting to die or hurt themselves
- Looking for ways to end their life
- Talking about feeling hopeless or having no purpose
- Expressing they're a burden to others
- Increasing use of alcohol or drugs
- Withdrawing from friends and activities
- Sleeping too much or too little
- Visiting or calling people to say goodbye
- Giving away prized possessions
- Aggression or reckless behavior

**Mood and Personality Changes:**
- Severe mood swings
- Sudden calmness after depression (may indicate a plan)
- Extreme anxiety or panic
- Intense irritability or rage
- Becoming withdrawn or disconnected
- Showing little emotion
- Dramatic personality changes

How to Help Someone:

**1. Express Concern:**
- Choose a private, comfortable setting
- Be direct: "I'm worried about you"
- Share specific observations
- Listen without judgment
- Don't minimize their feelings

**2. Ask Directly:**
- If you're concerned about suicide, ask: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
- Asking does NOT put the idea in their head
- Be prepared to listen to the answer
- Take all mentions seriously

**3. Provide Support:**
- Take their concerns seriously
- Don't promise to keep secrets about safety
- Stay with them if they're in crisis
- Remove access to means of harm if possible
- Help them connect with professional support

**4. Connect to Resources:**
- Offer to help find professional help
- Provide crisis hotline numbers
- Accompany them to appointments if willing
- Follow up regularly
- Encourage them to talk to trusted adults

What NOT to Do:

- Don't tell them to "just cheer up" or "think positive"
- Don't dismiss their feelings
- Don't promise to keep suicide plans secret
- Don't leave someone in immediate danger alone
- Don't try to handle a serious situation alone
- Don't judge or criticize
- Don't compare their problems to others'

When to Seek Different Types of Help:

**Primary Care Doctor:**
- Initial assessment of symptoms
- Rule out medical causes
- Medication management
- Referrals to specialists

**School Counselor:**
- Academic stress and pressure
- Social issues at school
- Initial mental health support
- Referrals to external support

**Therapist/Counselor:**
- Talk therapy for ongoing issues
- Learning coping strategies
- Working through trauma
- Relationship problems
- General mental health support

**Psychiatrist:**
- Medication evaluation
- Severe mental health conditions
- Diagnosis and treatment planning
- Medication management combined with therapy

**Emergency Services:**
- Immediate safety concerns
- Suicide attempts or plans
- Severe crisis situations
- Medical emergencies

Overcoming Barriers to Seeking Help:

**"I should be able to handle this myself"**
- Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness
- Mental health conditions are medical conditions
- No one expects you to treat a broken bone yourself

**"What will people think?"**
- Mental health is private and confidential
- More people struggle than you realize
- Your health matters more than stigma

**"I don't want to burden anyone"**
- Mental health professionals chose their field to help
- Friends and family want to support you
- Getting help early prevents bigger problems

**"It's too expensive"**
- Many affordable and free options exist in Singapore
- Polyclinics offer subsidized mental health services
- Community programs available
- Check insurance coverage

**"I don't know where to start"**
- Start with your school counselor or GP
- Call a helpline for guidance
- CHAT offers free assessment
- Community centers can direct you

Taking the First Step:

**Prepare for Your First Appointment:**
- Write down your symptoms and concerns
- Note when symptoms started
- List current medications
- Bring questions you have
- Be honest about what you're experiencing

**What to Expect:**
- Confidential conversation
- Questions about your experiences
- Discussion of treatment options
- Creation of a support plan
- Possible referrals to specialists

**Remember:**
- Recovery is possible
- You deserve support
- Help is available
- You're not alone
- Early intervention helps

Important Reminders:

✓ Mental health conditions are real medical conditions
✓ Treatment works - most people improve with proper support
✓ You don't have to hit rock bottom to ask for help
✓ Small problems are valid problems
✓ Prevention and early intervention are ideal
✓ You deserve to feel better

If you're reading this and recognizing warning signs in yourself or someone you care about, please reach out for help. It's one of the bravest and most important things you can do. Support is available, and recovery is possible.

In an emergency, always call 995 or go to the nearest emergency department. Your safety is the priority.
