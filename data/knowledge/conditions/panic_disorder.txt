# Panic Disorder - Complete Guide

## üß† What is Panic Disorder?

Panic disorder is a type of anxiety disorder characterized by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks and persistent worry about having future attacks. It's more than just feeling anxious - panic attacks involve intense physical symptoms that can feel like a medical emergency.

**Key Points:**
- Affects about 2-3% of people worldwide
- Often begins in late teens or early adulthood
- More common in women than men
- Highly treatable with proper care
- Can significantly impact quality of life if untreated

**Panic Attack vs Panic Disorder:**
- **Panic Attack**: Single episode of intense fear with physical symptoms
- **Panic Disorder**: Recurrent panic attacks + persistent worry about future attacks + significant life changes due to fear

## ‚ö° What is a Panic Attack?

A panic attack is a sudden surge of intense fear or discomfort that reaches peak intensity within minutes and includes multiple physical and emotional symptoms.

### Physical Symptoms
**Cardiovascular:**
- Racing or pounding heartbeat (palpitations)
- Chest pain or discomfort
- Feeling like heart is stopping or skipping beats

**Respiratory:**
- Shortness of breath or feeling of suffocation
- Hyperventilation (breathing too fast)
- Feeling like you can't catch your breath

**Neurological:**
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Feeling faint or like you might pass out
- Numbness or tingling in hands, feet, or face
- Trembling or shaking

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Nausea or stomach upset
- Feeling like you might vomit
- Butterflies or churning in stomach

**Other Physical:**
- Sweating or chills
- Hot flashes or cold flashes
- Feeling detached from your body

### Emotional Symptoms
- **Intense fear** that something terrible is happening
- **Fear of dying** - feeling like you're having a heart attack
- **Fear of losing control** - worry about "going crazy"
- **Fear of embarrassment** - worry about what others will think
- **Sense of unreality** - feeling like things aren't real (derealization)
- **Detachment** - feeling disconnected from yourself (depersonalization)

### Timeline of a Panic Attack
- **0-2 minutes**: Symptoms begin suddenly
- **2-10 minutes**: Symptoms reach peak intensity
- **10-30 minutes**: Symptoms gradually decrease
- **30+ minutes**: Person may feel tired, drained, or worried

**Important**: While extremely distressing, panic attacks are not dangerous and will pass on their own.

## üÜö Panic Attack vs Heart Attack vs Other Conditions

### How to Tell the Difference

#### Panic Attack Characteristics:
- **Age**: Often occurs in younger people (20s-40s)
- **Onset**: Sudden, peaks within 10 minutes
- **Duration**: Usually 5-30 minutes
- **Triggers**: Often no obvious trigger, or triggered by anxiety
- **Chest pain**: Sharp, stabbing, or aching
- **Other symptoms**: Multiple anxiety symptoms (sweating, trembling, fear)
- **Response to reassurance**: May improve with calming techniques

#### Heart Attack Characteristics:
- **Age**: More common in older people or those with risk factors
- **Onset**: May be gradual or sudden
- **Duration**: Pain persists or worsens
- **Triggers**: Often related to physical exertion
- **Chest pain**: Crushing, pressure, or squeezing sensation
- **Other symptoms**: May include jaw pain, arm pain, nausea
- **Response to reassurance**: Symptoms don't improve with calming

#### When to Seek Emergency Care:
**Call 995 immediately if:**
- Chest pain with crushing or squeezing sensation
- Pain radiating to jaw, neck, or arm
- Shortness of breath with chest pain
- Person has risk factors for heart disease
- First-time symptoms in someone over 40
- Symptoms lasting more than 30 minutes
- Person loses consciousness

**In Singapore**: When in doubt, go to the nearest hospital emergency department or call 995.

## üéØ Causes and Risk Factors

### Biological Factors
- **Genetics**: Family history increases risk by 3-5 times
- **Brain chemistry**: Imbalances in neurotransmitters (serotonin, GABA, norepinephrine)
- **Brain structure**: Differences in amygdala (fear center) and other regions
- **Medical conditions**: Thyroid problems, heart conditions, inner ear disorders
- **Substance use**: Caffeine, alcohol withdrawal, certain medications

### Psychological Factors
- **Anxiety sensitivity**: Fear of anxiety sensations themselves
- **Catastrophic thinking**: Tendency to interpret physical sensations as dangerous
- **Previous trauma**: Past frightening experiences
- **Stress sensitivity**: Higher reactivity to life stressors
- **Perfectionism**: Need for control and certainty

### Environmental Triggers
- **Major life changes**: Job loss, relationship changes, moving
- **Chronic stress**: Ongoing work, family, or financial pressure
- **Physical triggers**: Caffeine, lack of sleep, illness
- **Situational triggers**: Crowded places, enclosed spaces, bridges
- **Substance use**: Alcohol, drugs, even prescribed medications

### Singapore-Specific Risk Factors
- **Urban stress**: Crowded MRT trains, traffic, noise
- **Work pressure**: Long hours, competitive environment
- **Academic stress**: Exam pressure, fear of failure
- **Climate factors**: Heat and humidity can worsen physical symptoms
- **Cultural factors**: Pressure to maintain composure, fear of losing face

## üõ°Ô∏è Immediate Coping Strategies

### During a Panic Attack

#### Step 1: Recognize What's Happening
- **Remind yourself**: "This is a panic attack, not a medical emergency"
- **Remember**: "This will pass - panic attacks always end"
- **Stay present**: "I am safe right now"

#### Step 2: Control Your Breathing
**4-7-8 Breathing Technique:**
1. Exhale completely
2. Inhale through nose for 4 counts
3. Hold breath for 7 counts
4. Exhale through mouth for 8 counts
5. Repeat 3-4 times

**Box Breathing:**
1. Inhale for 4 counts
2. Hold for 4 counts
3. Exhale for 4 counts
4. Hold empty for 4 counts
5. Repeat until calm

#### Step 3: Ground Yourself
**5-4-3-2-1 Technique:**
- **5 things** you can see
- **4 things** you can touch
- **3 things** you can hear
- **2 things** you can smell
- **1 thing** you can taste

**Physical grounding:**
- Press feet firmly on ground
- Hold a cold object (water bottle, ice)
- Touch different textures around you

#### Step 4: Use Calming Self-Talk
**Helpful phrases:**
- "This feeling is uncomfortable but not dangerous"
- "I have survived panic attacks before"
- "This will pass in a few minutes"
- "I am safe right now"
- "I can handle this feeling"

#### Step 5: Stay Where You Are (if safe)
- **Don't run away** - this reinforces fear
- **Stay in the situation** if possible
- **Use coping strategies** while remaining present
- **Ask for support** from people nearby if needed

### After a Panic Attack

#### Immediate Aftermath (First Hour)
- **Rest**: Find a quiet, comfortable place
- **Hydrate**: Drink water to replace fluids lost through sweating
- **Breathe normally**: Don't continue deep breathing exercises
- **Be gentle**: Don't judge yourself for having the attack
- **Avoid caffeine**: Can increase anxiety in vulnerable period

#### Recovery (Next Few Hours)
- **Reflect**: What might have triggered the attack?
- **Plan**: What coping strategies worked or could work better?
- **Self-care**: Gentle activities like walking, warm bath, or relaxing music
- **Nutrition**: Eat regular, balanced meals
- **Connect**: Talk to supportive friends or family if helpful

## üè• Treatment Options in Singapore

### Professional Treatment

#### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
**Most effective treatment for panic disorder**

**How CBT helps:**
- **Psychoeducation**: Understanding panic attacks and anxiety
- **Cognitive restructuring**: Changing catastrophic thoughts
- **Exposure therapy**: Gradually facing feared situations
- **Interoceptive exposure**: Learning to tolerate physical sensations
- **Relapse prevention**: Maintaining gains long-term

**What to expect:**
- **Duration**: Usually 12-20 sessions
- **Homework**: Practice exercises between sessions
- **Progress**: Most people see improvement within 6-12 weeks
- **Success rate**: 70-80% of people experience significant improvement

**Cost in Singapore:**
- **Public sector**: $30-60 per session with subsidies
- **CHAT** (ages 16-30): Free or low-cost
- **Private**: $150-300 per session

#### Medication Options
**Available through psychiatrists at IMH, polyclinics, or private practice**

**Antidepressants (First-line treatment):**
- **SSRIs**: Sertraline, Escitalopram, Fluoxetine
  - **Timeline**: 4-6 weeks for full effect
  - **Benefits**: Reduce frequency and severity of attacks
  - **Side effects**: Initial increase in anxiety (temporary), nausea, headache

- **SNRIs**: Venlafaxine, Duloxetine
  - **Timeline**: 4-6 weeks for full effect
  - **Benefits**: Good for panic with depression
  - **Side effects**: Similar to SSRIs, may affect blood pressure

**Anti-anxiety medications (Short-term use):**
- **Benzodiazepines**: Lorazepam, Clonazepam, Alprazolam
  - **Timeline**: Work within 30-60 minutes
  - **Benefits**: Rapid relief during attacks
  - **Cautions**: Risk of dependence, drowsiness, memory issues
  - **Usage**: Usually prescribed for 2-4 weeks only

**Cost in Singapore:**
- **Public sector**: $10-25 per month with subsidies
- **Private**: $80-200 per month
- **Medisave**: Can be used for psychiatric medications

## üè† Self-Help and Lifestyle Management

### Daily Prevention Strategies

#### Lifestyle Modifications
- **Regular exercise**: 30 minutes daily, even walking helps
- **Sleep hygiene**: 7-9 hours nightly, consistent schedule
- **Limit caffeine**: Especially if you're sensitive
- **Reduce alcohol**: Can worsen anxiety and interfere with sleep
- **Balanced diet**: Regular meals, avoid blood sugar spikes
- **Stay hydrated**: Dehydration can trigger physical symptoms

#### Stress Management
- **Time management**: Reduce overwhelming schedules
- **Relaxation techniques**: Daily practice when calm
- **Mindfulness**: Present-moment awareness
- **Problem-solving**: Address stressors systematically
- **Social support**: Maintain connections with supportive people

#### Singapore-Specific Strategies
- **Climate management**: Stay cool, use air conditioning, dress appropriately
- **Transport planning**: Travel during off-peak hours when possible
- **Space awareness**: Know where exits are in crowded places
- **Cultural balance**: Manage pressure to appear perfect or in control

### Building Panic Resistance

#### Exposure Exercises
**Gradual exposure to feared sensations:**

**Heart rate elevation:**
- Walk up stairs
- Do jumping jacks
- Run in place
- Use exercise bike

**Breathing changes:**
- Hold breath for 30 seconds
- Breathe through a straw
- Hyperventilate briefly (supervised)

**Dizziness induction:**
- Spin in a chair
- Stand up quickly
- Look up and turn head side to side

**Important**: Start with low-intensity exercises and gradually increase. Practice when calm, not during panic attacks.

#### Cognitive Exercises
**Challenging panic-related thoughts:**

**Common panic thought**: "I'm having a heart attack"
**Challenge**: "I've had this checked - my heart is healthy. This is anxiety."
**Balanced thought**: "This is uncomfortable but not dangerous"

**Common panic thought**: "I'm going to faint"
**Challenge**: "People rarely faint from panic attacks - blood pressure actually goes up"
**Balanced thought**: "I feel dizzy but I'm not in danger of fainting"

**Common panic thought**: "Everyone will think I'm crazy"
**Challenge**: "Most people can't tell when someone is having a panic attack"
**Balanced thought**: "Even if people notice, it doesn't define who I am"

## üö´ Agoraphobia and Avoidance

### Understanding Agoraphobia
Agoraphobia often develops after panic attacks, involving fear of situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable if a panic attack occurs.

### Common Avoided Situations
- **Transportation**: MRT, buses, taxis, airplanes
- **Enclosed spaces**: Elevators, theaters, small rooms
- **Crowded places**: Shopping malls, hawker centers, markets
- **Open spaces**: Large parking lots, bridges, parks
- **Being alone**: Away from home or safe person

### Singapore-Specific Avoidance Patterns
- **MRT avoidance**: Especially during peak hours or in tunnels
- **Shopping mall avoidance**: Crowded, enclosed environments
- **Work avoidance**: Fear of panic attacks during meetings or presentations
- **Social avoidance**: Declining invitations due to fear of panic
- **Independence loss**: Relying on others for transportation or errands

### Breaking the Avoidance Cycle

#### Gradual Exposure Plan
**Example: MRT avoidance**
1. **Week 1**: Look at MRT maps and photos online
2. **Week 2**: Drive/walk past MRT station
3. **Week 3**: Enter MRT station, don't travel
4. **Week 4**: Take one stop during off-peak hours
5. **Week 5**: Take two stops with support person
6. **Week 6**: Take longer journeys, eventually alone

**Key principles:**
- Start with easiest step
- Stay in situation until anxiety decreases by half
- Use coping strategies during exposure
- Gradually increase difficulty
- Practice regularly

### How Panic Disorder Affects Relationships

#### Common Relationship Impacts
- **Dependency**: Relying on others for support or transportation
- **Social isolation**: Declining invitations, avoiding social events
- **Communication difficulties**: Hard to explain panic attacks to others
- **Misunderstanding**: Others may not understand severity of symptoms business impacts**
- **Avoidance of work travel or meetings**
- **Reduced productivity during anxiety periods**
- **Fear of panic attacks affecting performance**

### Supporting Someone with Panic Disorder

#### For Family Members and Friends

**Do:**
- **Learn about panic disorder** - understand it's a real medical condition
- **Stay calm** during their panic attacks
- **Encourage treatment** - support their decision to seek help
- **Be patient** - recovery takes time
- **Maintain normal activities** - don't rearrange your whole life
- **Practice breathing exercises** with them when they're calm

**Don't:**
- **Tell them to "just relax"** - panic attacks aren't voluntary
- **Avoid all trigger situations** - this reinforces avoidance
- **Take on all responsibilities** - they need to maintain independence
- **Get frustrated** - remember they're not choosing to feel this way
- **Assume they're "faking it"** - panic attacks are very real

#### Supporting Recovery
- **Encourage gradual exposure** - celebrate small steps
- **Provide transportation initially** - but work toward independence
- **Learn their coping strategies** - help them practice when calm
- **Maintain social connections** - gently encourage social activities
- **Take care of yourself** - supporting someone with panic disorder can be stressful

### Singapore Family Considerations
- **Cultural understanding**: Help family understand panic disorder isn't weakness
- **Language barriers**: Ensure all family members understand the condition
- **Traditional beliefs**: Balance cultural approaches with modern treatment
- **Extended family**: Educate broader family network about panic disorder
- **Stigma management**: Address shame or embarrassment about mental health

## üåü Recovery and Long-term Management

### What Recovery Looks Like
**Recovery doesn't mean never having anxiety again, but:**
- **Reduced frequency**: Panic attacks occur less often
- **Less intensity**: When anxiety occurs, it's more manageable
- **Faster recovery**: Panic attacks resolve more quickly
- **Increased confidence**: Growing belief in ability to handle anxiety
- **Functional improvement**: Can engage in previously avoided activities
- **Quality of life**: Return to normal work, social, and family activities

### Maintaining Progress

#### Ongoing Strategies
- **Continue coping skills practice** - even when feeling better
- **Regular exercise and stress management**
- **Maintain therapy gains** - periodic booster sessions
- **Monitor medication** - work with doctor on long-term plan
- **Address new stressors** - don't let stress build up

#### Preventing Relapse
**Warning signs to watch for:**
- Increasing avoidance of previously mastered situations
- Return of catastrophic thinking patterns
- Increased use of safety behaviors
- Avoiding coping strategy practice
- Major life stressors without adequate support

**Action steps for warning signs:**
- **Return to basics** - practice fundamental coping skills
- **Seek support** - contact therapist or support group
- **Address stressors** - don't ignore mounting pressures
- **Medication review** - consult with prescribing doctor
- **Self-care renewal** - return to healthy lifestyle habits

### Long-term Outlook
- **85-90%** of people with panic disorder improve significantly with treatment
- **Many people recover completely** and rarely think about panic attacks
- **Some people** have occasional mild anxiety but can manage it effectively
- **Relapse is possible** but doesn't mean returning to square one
- **Early treatment** leads to better long-term outcomes

## üí° Myths vs Facts

### Myth: "Panic attacks can cause heart attacks"
**Fact**: Panic attacks don't cause heart attacks. While the symptoms feel similar, panic attacks don't damage the heart.

### Myth: "If you have panic disorder, you'll always have it"
**Fact**: Many people recover completely from panic disorder with proper treatment. Others learn to manage it so effectively it rarely impacts their lives.

### Myth: "Panic attacks mean you're going crazy"
**Fact**: Panic attacks are anxiety responses, not signs of mental instability. People having panic attacks are fully aware of reality.

### Myth: "You should avoid anything that triggers panic attacks"
**Fact**: Avoidance actually makes panic disorder worse. Gradual exposure to triggers is key to recovery.

### Myth: "Medication is the only effective treatment"
**Fact**: Therapy, especially CBT, is often more effective long-term than medication alone. Many people recover with therapy only.

### Myth: "Panic attacks are just severe nervousness"
**Fact**: Panic attacks involve intense physical symptoms and overwhelming fear that goes far beyond normal nervousness.

## üÜò When to Seek Professional Help

### Immediate Professional Help Needed
**Contact mental health professional or go to emergency if:**
- **Frequent panic attacks** - more than once per week
- **Significant avoidance** - unable to work, study, or maintain relationships
- **Depression symptoms** - feeling hopeless, suicidal thoughts
- **Substance use** - using alcohol or drugs to cope
- **Physical symptoms** - chest pain, shortness of breath needing medical clearance

### Regular Professional Help Recommended
**Contact CHAT (6493-6500) or your GP for referral if:**
- **Panic attacks persist** for more than a month
- **Lifestyle impact** - avoiding important activities
- **Relationship strain** - family or work relationships suffering
- **Self-help not working** - tried coping strategies without improvement for 6-8 weeks
- **Anxiety about anxiety** - constant worry about future panic attacks

### Emergency Situations
**Call 995 or go to emergency department if:**
- **First-time symptoms** in someone over 40 or with heart disease risk
- **Chest pain** with crushing sensation, arm pain, or jaw pain
- **Breathing difficulties** that don't improve with calming techniques
- **Loss of consciousness** or severe dizziness
- **Symptoms lasting** more than 30 minutes without improvement

## üåü Final Message

If you're struggling with panic attacks or panic disorder, remember:

**You are not alone** - millions of people worldwide experience panic disorder
**It's not your fault** - panic disorder is a medical condition, not a personal failure
**Recovery is possible** - with proper treatment, most people improve significantly
**You are brave** - seeking help and facing your fears takes tremendous courage
**This will get better** - panic disorder is highly treatable

Take the first step by reaching out for professional help. In Singapore, CHAT (6493-6500) is an excellent starting point for young adults, or speak with your GP about mental health referral options.

**Remember**: Panic attacks feel terrible but are not dangerous. With proper treatment and coping strategies, you can learn to manage and overcome panic disorder.
